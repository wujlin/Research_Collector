## Abstract

Urban sprawl reshapes cities, yet its quantitative laws remain elusive. Analyzing built-up expansion in 19 cities from 1985 to 2015 with tools from surface growth physics in radial geometry, the authors reveal anisotropic, branch-like urban growth and a piecewise linear relation between built-up area and population. They identify a robust local roughness exponent, approximately `alpha_loc = 0.54`, alongside variable growth and dynamic exponents. This coexistence of one robust local exponent with variable global exponents makes urban expansion a useful empirical test bed for nonequilibrium growth theory.

## Why It Matters

This paper is directly connected to the current urban-scaling reading line. The earlier city-growth work focuses on population dynamics and interurban migration shocks; this paper moves the object from city population size to the spatial boundary of urban expansion. The key shift is from "how city populations grow" to "how the built-up urban frontier roughens over time."

Methodologically, it imports the language of surface growth physics into urban expansion: roughness, radial growth, local scaling exponents, anisotropic branches, and nonequilibrium morphology. That makes it a bridge between urban complex systems and statistical-physics growth models.

## Key Contributions

- Introduces a surface-growth framing for urban sprawl, treating the expanding built-up boundary as a roughening front.
- Studies 19 cities over 1985-2015 and finds anisotropic, branch-like expansion rather than isotropic radial growth.
- Reports a robust local roughness exponent around `alpha_loc = 0.54`.
- Shows that this local universality coexists with city-dependent global exponents, so urban expansion is not captured by a single universal scaling law.
- Connects empirical urban morphology to nonequilibrium growth theory and sets up a modeling target for future urban-sprawl dynamics.
