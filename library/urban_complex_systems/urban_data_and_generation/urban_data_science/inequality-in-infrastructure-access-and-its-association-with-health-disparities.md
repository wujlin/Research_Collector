## Abstract

The paper constructs global gridded measures of economic, social, and environmental infrastructure access, then compares population-weighted access and inequality across 166 countries. It links infrastructure access and access inequality to health outcomes, showing that Global South countries face lower infrastructure access and higher inequality, and that infrastructure inequality is associated with worse health outcomes, especially for economic infrastructure.

## Key Contributions

- Builds a harmonized global infrastructure access assessment across economic, social, and environmental dimensions.
- Uses population-weighted exposure rather than only aggregate infrastructure stock, making the measure closer to actual human access.
- Connects infrastructure access inequality with health metrics such as HALE and DALYs.
- Provides a policy-relevant empirical bridge between infrastructure distribution, spatial inequality, sustainable development, and public health.
